# Corviz — Post-Pilot Sequence
### 3 emails · 30 days · Customers who took the refund

---

## Sequence overview

**Who receives this:** Pilot customers who chose the refund at day 90.

**Trigger:** Manual — Jack sends P1 on the same day the refund is processed. Do not automate this sequence. Each email should be sent by Jack personally.

**Purpose:** Gather honest feedback. Leave the relationship in good standing. Keep the door open without pressure.

**What this sequence is not:** A re-engagement campaign. A win-back sequence. An attempt to change their mind. None of these emails should feel like another sales attempt. If they do, rewrite them.

## P1 — Refund processed

**Send:** Day 91 — same day as refund confirmation (manual send)
**Subject:** Refund processed — and three questions
**Preview text:** Done. No hard feelings.

---

Hi [First name],

Refund processed. £99 back in your account within 3 to 5 working days depending on your bank.

No hard feelings. The guarantee was real from day one and I meant it.

One favour, if you're willing — three questions:

1. What didn't work? Was it the reminders themselves, the data quality, the timing, the setup — or something else?

2. Was there a moment during the pilot where you decided it wasn't going to work for you? When was it?

3. Is there anything I could have done differently — the onboarding, the first batch, the check-ins — that would have changed the outcome?

Reply to this email. You don't have to answer all three. One honest answer is more useful than three polite ones.

Jack
Founder, Corviz

P.S. Your customer data has been deleted from the Corviz system. If you'd like a copy of your exported data before it's gone, reply and I'll send it over.

---

**Word count:** 162

**Send notes:**
- Send on the same day the refund is processed — not the following week
- Read every reply personally. These are the most valuable inputs in the business — they tell Jack exactly what to fix before the next pilot customer
- Do not follow up if no reply to P1. P2 handles that.
- The data deletion line is important — it shows respect for the customer's data and pre-empts any GDPR concern

## P2 — One question

**Send:** Day 100 — 9 days after P1 (manual send)
**Subject:** One question
**Preview text:** If one thing were different.

---

Hi [First name],

Short one.

If one thing about the pilot had been different — the setup, the timing, the product, anything at all — would you have continued?

Reply with whatever comes to mind. Doesn't have to be long.

Jack

P.S. No agenda here. I'm not trying to sell you back in. I want to understand what the single biggest obstacle was.

---

**Word count:** 57

**Conditional send — read before sending:**
- Only send P2 if the customer did not reply to P1 and did not submit the feedback form on the refund confirmation page
- If they replied to P1 with genuine feedback, P2 is redundant — skip it
- If they replied to P1 with a short or partial answer, P2 can still go — it asks a different, narrower question
- The P.S. is load-bearing. "No agenda" needs to be true — do not add any offer or CTA to this email under any circumstances

**Why this question works:** "If one thing were different" is easier to answer than "what didn't work." It invites the customer to imagine a better version rather than catalogue a failure. The replies are often more honest and more specific.

**Tone benchmark for this sequence:** The right tone is the same tone you'd use with someone you genuinely respect who tried something and it didn't work out. Not apologetic. Not salesy. Not cold. Just honest and decent.

## What to do with the feedback

Replies to this sequence are product research. Every answer to the three questions in P1 is a signal about what to fix before the next pilot.

After each refund, block 30 minutes to read the feedback and write one specific improvement to take into the next customer's pilot. Do not let feedback accumulate — act on each piece while the pilot is still fresh.

Track refund reasons in a simple spreadsheet: customer, date, primary reason given, secondary reason, what you would do differently. By the time you have 3 refunds, a pattern will have emerged. That pattern is the most important thing in the business at that point.
