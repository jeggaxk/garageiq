# COPYWRITING_SKILL.md
## Direct Response Copywriting — Agent Reference Guide

Frameworks extracted from "Learn Copywriting in 76 Minutes" with Harry Dry. This skill governs the craft of writing itself — the sentence level, the word level, the structure level. Use alongside MARKETING_SKILL.md (which governs strategy) and OFFER_SKILL.md (which governs what you're selling). This skill governs how you say it.

## THE THREE-QUESTION TEST
Run every sentence through this before it ships. Three yeses = strong. Three nos = rewrite.

**1. Can I visualise it?**
If you close your eyes and cannot see it, it is not there yet. Abstract = forgettable. Concrete = memorable. You cannot drop "a better way" on your foot. You can drop a musly Irishman.

**2. Can I falsify it?**
Is the sentence true or false? If it cannot be proved or disproved, it is just noise. Falsifiable copy makes people sit up — it puts your head on the chopping block and signals you believe what you are saying.

**3. Can nobody else say it?**
If a competitor could sign this ad, it should not exist. Good copy is bespoke. It can only come from you, your product, your specific truth. Never write an ad a competitor can sign.

**Quick application:**
- "Don't just get a job. Change an entire industry." — fails all three. Abstract, unfalsifiable, anyone can say it.
- "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio." — passes all three. Visual, true, only New Balance can say it.

## THE THREE-PART COPYWRITING FRAMEWORK

Every piece of copy has three jobs. Do not skip any of them.

**Part 1: Who are you talking to?**
- Define one specific person. Not a demographic — a moment. Where are they? What are they doing? What do they already believe?
- The current attitude is the starting point. The desired attitude is the finish line. Your copy strings the wire between the two telephone poles.
- If you do not know who you are talking to, your copy will talk to no one.
- Mistake: Snapchat ran a Super Bowl ad targeting people who use Snapchat. The average Super Bowl viewer is 39. Know your audience's context, not just their profile.
- Test: put your copy in the context in which it will actually be read. Billboard surrounded by seven others. Email in a crowded inbox. Phone screen on a train. Does it still work?

**Part 2: You have to have something to say**
- Copy is not just saying it well. You need a real point of view, a belief, a truth that you actually stand behind.
- The best copy comes from people who built something because they believed something was wrong with the world. That conviction shows.
- If you are writing for something you do not believe in, it will read like it.
- Hinge: "The dating app designed to be deleted." That is a belief, not a feature.

**Part 3: Say it well**
- This is the craft — the actual words. Most people think this is all copywriting is. It is only the third part, and it only works if parts one and two are solid.

## HOW TO GO FROM ABSTRACT TO CONCRETE

The zoom-in method:
1. Write the abstract word or phrase at the top of a page
2. Ask: what do I actually mean?
3. Keep zooming in until you land on a concrete object, a specific person, a real number, a visual scene

**Examples:**
- "Regain fitness" → getting off the couch → running → 5K → Couch to 5K
- "Pretty people in big cities" → supermodels in London
- "Old people in non-big cities" → dads in Ohio
- "Good values" → reads on the tube, 6'2, looks like Ryan Gosling

**The pointing rule:** you cannot talk, you can only point. Do not describe gold as a great investment — point at the 50-year chart going one way. Do not say someone is funny — point at the thing they did that was funny. Get off the adjective trail entirely.

## FALSIFIABILITY IN PRACTICE

**The blind date test:**
- Subjective description: "He's good-looking, intelligent, funny, great values." — nobody believes this.
- Falsifiable description: "Looks like Ryan Gosling. 6'2. Reads on the tube. Works in finance." — now I can picture him. Now I believe you.

**Facts as the foundation:**
- Facts ground everything. They guarantee you are saying something real.
- A fact does not have to be numerical — it just has to be true and specific.
- "Asana spends £345 million on sales and marketing." That is a fact. Build from it.
- Bad: "There's a lot of inequality." Good: show the graph. Point at the number.
- In doubt — give a fact. Then make something with it.

**Precision signals credibility:**
- "It takes 3.1 seconds to read this ad — the same time it takes a Model S to go 0 to 60." Precise numbers feel true. Round numbers feel estimated.
- Expanding time frames makes small numbers bigger: "4 hours a day writing" → "22,000 hours in your career."

## THE ONE MISSISSIPPI TEST

Show the copy to someone. One Mississippi, two Mississippi. Do they get it?

If it takes longer than 2 seconds to land — rewrite. If they do not get it at all — rewrite. If everyone gets it too easily, you may not be doing something interesting enough. The goal is instant for the right person, not universal.

## CONFLICT AS THE ENGINE OF COPY

Almost every great piece of copy has conflict at its centre. Conflict creates contrast. Contrast creates memory.

**Three types of enemy/conflict:**
- A — different approaches (the corporate way vs the copywriting way)
- B — beliefs (I believe this, you believe that)
- C — competitors (Apple vs PC, Corvette vs Volvo)

**Before and after is conflict.** Problem and solution is conflict. What it costs you now vs what it could cost you with this product is conflict.

**The pickle juice and orange juice principle:** humans remember things relatively. Show the bad version first so the good version tastes better. Show how every other screen recorder positions itself — then show how Loom does it differently. The contrast makes the good thing better.

**Structuring conflict with "but":**
Boring: "I got on the train. I had a coffee. We started talking."
Conflict: "I woke up at 8:52. My mum wouldn't let me leave the house in my shorts. I got here. Now you're asking me why I'm wearing trousers."
The word "but" (or the idea of it) creates the turn. What went wrong? That is where the story lives.

## WRITING SIMPLY

**Kaplan's Law of Words:** any word that is not working for you is working against you. If in doubt, cut it.

**Harry's Law:** you are not taking Kaplan's Law seriously enough.

Simple writing is not written simply — it is rewritten simply. You cannot write it simple. You can only rewrite it simple.

**Practical rules:**
- Never write a paragraph longer than two lines in short-form copy
- Short paragraphs are easy to swing between — like monkey bars
- A long paragraph usually means you are not explaining yourself as well as you could
- Give good lines room to breathe — white space is not wasted space

**The burrito test for paragraphs:** a good paragraph should hold together when you throw it. Pull one sentence out — if the paragraph still works without it, the sentence should not have been there.

**Rewriting method:**
- Write it. Copy and paste. Write it a different way. Repeat 4–5 times.
- The freedom to do it wrong is what lets you do it right.
- Show multiple versions to someone else — they can say "that one" much more easily than "is this good?"

**Grade level:** write at a 5th–8th grade reading level. Not because your reader is unintelligent — because clarity at that level resonates more deeply than cleverness at a higher one.

## STRUCTURE

Good structure makes complex things holdable. It gives the reader something to grip.

**Dividing lines and parallelism:**
- Break big things into two or three named parts
- "Part one: things that don't scale. Part two: momentum." That is structure.
- "The corporate way. The copywriting way." That is structure.
- Parallelism means the parts sound and feel the same in form — even if they are opposites in meaning

**The "that's interesting" framework:**
- Take two things people think are different — show they are actually one
- Take one thing — show it is actually two
- This is how you create structure that surprises

**A book without chapters is long and heavy.** Chapters are structure. Give your copy chapters — even if they are invisible ones.

## THE AD CREATION PROCESS (Harry's Method)

1. **Find the seed** — one true, interesting fact or observation. Write it down. Do not write the ad yet.
2. **Find the conflict** — draw a line down the page. Write what is on the left. Write what is on the right. What are the two opposites here?
3. **Write a rough version** — do not try to make it good. Get the dirty water out of the tap first.
4. **Iterate 20–25 times** — each pass, ask: is every word earning its place? Can I make this one line? Can I make it parallel? Can I make it shorter?
5. **Solve one problem at a time** — fixing one thing often breaks another. That is normal. Keep going.
6. **Test the visual layout** — design and copy work together. Put the copy in the context it will actually live in. Does the hierarchy guide the eye correctly?
7. **Ship when you cannot remove anything** — done is not "I like this." Done is "I cannot take anything away from this and make it better."

## STANDARDS

Your standards are your work. Your work is nothing but your standards.

- Nine out of 10 things should not go out. That is not perfectionism — that is the filter that makes the one thing that does go out worth reading.
- You know it is done when you take a walk, come back, and cannot remove anything. It is as clean as it is ever going to get.
- A great line: you cannot add a word and make it better. You cannot take away a word and make it better. It just is.

## THE ROLE OF AI IN COPYWRITING

AI is a tool. It is only as good as your taste.

- Switching from pens to typewriters did not make writing better. AI will not either — unless the writer has taste, conviction, and experience.
- AI is predictive. It lays words out in fashions which have been laid out before. A good writer arranges words in a way that has not been laid out before.
- What AI cannot do: see something, believe something, write about it from lived experience. "What do you believe?" AI does not believe anything. That is why it cannot surprise you.
- What AI is useful for: research, generating rough variations to react to, getting dirty water out of the tap faster.
- Use it as a starting point, not a finishing point. Your taste, conviction, and experience do the real work.

## AGENT OPERATING PRINCIPLES

When writing any copy, the agent must:

1. **Run the three-question test** on every headline and hook before finalising — visualisable, falsifiable, ownable
2. **Define the one person** being spoken to before writing the first word — their context, their current attitude, their desired attitude
3. **Start with a fact or concrete observation** — never open with an abstraction or adjective
4. **Build conflict in** — identify the two sides before writing, use the before/after or enemy structure
5. **Zoom in on every abstract word** — use the zoom-in method until every claim is concrete and visual
6. **Apply Kaplan's Law** on every pass — cut any word not earning its place
7. **Rewrite at least 3 times** before presenting — the first version is the dirty water
8. **Test with the One Mississippi rule** — if it does not land in 2 seconds, rewrite the hook
9. **Never write an ad a competitor can sign** — if it is not ownable, it is not done
10. **Maintain standards** — do not present copy that cannot pass "I cannot remove anything from this and make it better"
