## WHY EMAIL FIRST

Email delivers 35–45x ROI on every pound spent on software and writing. The lead has already been acquired — email is just follow-up. One writer, one send, unlimited recipients. 61% of people check email multiple times per day. It works across all age groups and geographies equally.

The most common mistake is not doing it at all. The second most common mistake is doing it badly because you are afraid of unsubscribes. Both are just saying no to money.

Email has one of two purposes:
1. Increase the likelihood this person purchases in the future
2. Get them to purchase now

Everything else — tone, format, length, timing — serves one of those two purposes.

## PART 1: INDIVIDUAL EMAIL STRUCTURE

### The Core Email Format

Every email follows the same structure. Consistency builds a brand. People should know what they are going to get before they open it — like McDonald's. The burger changes but the experience does not.

**1. Subject line**
- Either builds the brand of the newsletter over time (consistent named series) or uses proof-over-promise for immediate opens
- AB test subject lines — send to a small segment, let the winner emerge, send the rest the winning version
- Never change the subject line strategy every email — pick one and build it

**2. Immediate reward (opening lines)**
- The first thing they see after opening must reward them for clicking
- A quote, a stat, a tweet, a one-liner — something with standalone value they get in one glance
- Behaviour is driven by what happened after the last time they did it — if opening your email felt rewarding before, they open again. If it did not, they stop.

**3. Meat of the email (the tactical content)**
- One concept only — do not try to cover multiple things
- Under 200 words for tactical emails — roughly one minute to read
- Introduce a problem or condition that makes the solution relevant, then deliver the solution
- Do not put the whole blog or resource in the email — put the most interesting nugget and link to the rest
- This trains the audience to click and rewards them for doing so

**4. CTA (call to action)**
- One CTA per email — one link, one action
- Make it contextual — the CTA must be directly related to the content of that email
- Do not paste in a generic CTA — take 30 seconds to make it feel like a natural next step from what they just read
- Wrong: "Check out our workshop" after an email about testimonials
- Right: "Want more tactics like this for making strangers trust you faster? Here is where we go deeper"

**5. PS statement**
- The PS is the second most read part of any email after the subject line
- Never skip it
- Use it as either a second CTA, a meme, a piece of social proof, or a teaser for the next email
- It is read even by people who scan and skip the body

### Email Design Principles

**Look like a real email, not a promotion**
The goal is to approximate the emails people exchange every day — mostly text, a handful of sentences, maybe one link. The moment an email looks like a Vegas billboard it gets filtered to the promotions tab and nobody reads it.

Rules:
- Plain text format wherever possible
- Maximum one to two links per email
- No heavy image use unless you are e-commerce — images get distorted and trigger spam filters
- Remove money language from the email body — words like "buy", "sale", "discount", "offer" increase the likelihood of landing in the promo tab
- Always check how the email looks on mobile — 58–60% of email is read on mobile

**Get people to reply**
Replies are the single best signal to email servers that your content is valued. When someone replies, you get moved out of the promo tab and into the primary inbox permanently for that recipient.

Use this intentionally — ask a question, request a yes reply to receive a bonus, create a reason for engagement. This is not just a deliverability trick — it is also the highest quality signal of actual interest.

**Preview text**
The text that appears after the subject line in the inbox view. 24%+ of people read this before deciding whether to open. If you do not set it manually it defaults to the first 150 characters of the email. Always set it deliberately — treat it as an extension of the subject line.

### Deliverability — Getting Into the Inbox

17% of marketing emails never reach the inbox at all. Domain authority and sender reputation determine whether you land in primary, promotions, or spam.

**What builds domain authority:**
- High open rates — people opening your emails signals quality
- High click rates — people clicking signals engagement
- Replies — the strongest positive signal
- Low unsubscribe rates — but only because the list is genuinely engaged, not because you made unsubscribing hard

**What destroys domain authority:**
- Sending to dormant or disengaged segments
- Making unsubscribing difficult — people who cannot unsubscribe just stop opening, which tanks your metrics worse than the unsubscribe would have
- Too many links in a single email
- Heavy money language in the body
- Sending to people who never open

**Advanced deliverability tactic:**
Use Google-native links where possible — YouTube links, Google Drive hosted assets, Google Docs. Email servers understand these formats and are more likely to let them through.

**Make unsubscribing easy:**
Counterintuitive but correct. Keeping uninterested people on the list tanks deliverability for the people who do want to read. A clean, engaged list outperforms a large, disengaged one every time.

### Segmentation

Segmentation produces a 791% increase in email ROI according to HubSpot research. The reason is simple — the right email to the right person converts. The wrong email to the right person does not.

Segment on whatever qualifiers matter for the offer:
- Revenue level or business stage
- Whether they have bought before (buyers vs non-buyers)
- What they opted in for
- Their answers to qualification questions

Never send an advanced email to beginners. Never send a beginner email to advanced people. Both groups ignore content that is not relevant to them and your metrics drop.

---

### Timing

**B2C (business to consumer):**
- Best days: Monday and Tuesday
- Average open rate increase of 18% vs other days
- Best times: 10am–12pm and 1pm–3pm

**B2B (business to business):**
- Best day: Wednesday
- Similar lift to B2C Monday/Tuesday
- Avoid Monday morning — business people are clearing actual work emails

For cross-timezone lists these specifics matter less. For single-market local businesses they matter significantly.

---

### Cadence

Start with once a month. Write 12 emails in one sitting — half a day — and you have a year of email done.

Quality threshold over calendar commitment: send when you have something genuinely valuable, not because a schedule says to. One excellent email beats four mediocre ones. A single bad email after four good ones can undo the goodwill of all four.

What kills long-term email performance: sending something bad because you felt obligated to send something. What builds it: consistently providing a positive return on the reader's time.

## PART 2: THE QUARTERLY CAMPAIGN FRAMEWORK

### The Email List as a Long-Term Asset

Ads are a paycheck — you work, you get paid, you stop working, it stops. Email is an investment account — you build it over time and it compounds. Sales from email drop straight to the bottom line because the cost to acquire the customer on email is zero — you already paid to get them on the list.

A well-managed email list also smooths business volatility. Ad performance can drop overnight. A list of 500,000 people does not.

Every business with a list should be running a conversion campaign against it at least once per quarter. Most businesses leave this money sitting entirely untouched.

---

### The Quarterly Cleanup Campaign

Once per quarter, run a full re-engagement and conversion campaign against the list. The goal is to take non-buyers and buyers who have not purchased a specific offer and convert them using a fresh mechanism.

**Why quarterly:**
- Provides regular revenue from an already-paid-for asset
- Keeps the list warm and engaged between campaigns
- Allows you to test and improve the campaign each time before blowing it to the full list

**The framework:**

**Step 1 — Choose or create a lead magnet**
The best lead magnet for a quarterly campaign is a challenge. A challenge works because it satisfies all four elements of the value equation simultaneously — clear outcome, high perceived likelihood of success, fast timeframe (7 days), and low perceived effort (structured steps provided).

A challenge also approximates a brand relationship rapidly — it crams six months of interaction into seven days. For cold or disengaged contacts who do not know you well enough to buy, this is how you earn the right to ask.

The lead magnet should be a complete solution to a narrow problem that naturally creates the next problem — which your main offer solves.

**Step 2 — Test on 10–15% of the list first**
Never blast the full list with an untested campaign. Send to a small segment, measure the metrics, identify what to improve, then roll out to the rest. Every improvement you make on the test segment compounds across the full list.

What to test: subject line, email opening, CTA copy, landing page headline, challenge or lead magnet name.

**Step 3 — Wrap it seasonally**
The same core offer can be run every quarter without fatiguing the list if you change the wrapper. The challenge is the same — the name, the landing page, and the framing change to match the season or a relevant life moment.

Examples from fitness: 6-Week Challenge → 42-Day Fix → Slim for Summer → Lean by Halloween → Slim for Santa. Same programme. Different wrapper. Same audience. No fatigue.

Once you have four tested seasonal wrappers for the year, they become an autopilot quarterly cash machine.

**Step 4 — Deposit Goodwill before withdrawing**
The give-to-ask ratio governs how often you can make an offer. Every offer you make costs Goodwill. Every piece of genuine value you provide deposits it.

You can make as many offers as you want as long as you have earned the right through deposits. The mistake is withdrawing without depositing — asking without giving first.

Between quarterly campaigns, provide value-only emails. No asks. Build the balance back up.

**Step 5 — It is okay to ask again**
People get busy. They leave checkouts open. They intend to buy and forget. Asking again is not harassment — it is a reminder. The second and third ask will convert people who genuinely wanted to buy but did not on the first one.

The rule: you can ask as long as you have earned the right. If the give-to-ask ratio is healthy, ask freely.

### Campaign Benchmarks (Real Numbers)

From a 464,000-person sub-segment campaign:
- Email open rate: 18%
- Click-through to landing page: 5% of openers
- Landing page to challenge registration: high percentage of clickers
- Challenge registrants to buyers: 8%
- Ascension to upsell: 20% of buyers
- Revenue per email on the list: £6
- Total campaign revenue: £2.4 million in 5 days

These are benchmarks for a warm but largely non-buyer list. Use them as direction, not as guaranteed outcomes.

### The Welcome Sequence — Onboarding New Optins

The moment someone opts in is the moment they are most likely to buy. Do not waste it.

**Welcome email structure:**

Email 1 (immediate):
- Deliver the lead magnet or confirm access
- Before giving them the thing they came for, sell them on why staying subscribed is worth it — what they will keep getting, what makes this list different
- Ask them to reply to confirm receipt — this gets you out of the promo tab immediately
- Give the lead magnet after they reply — this creates engagement and reinforces the inbox relationship

The reason to delay the lead magnet slightly: you have their maximum attention right now. Use it to sell the ongoing relationship before you give them the immediate reward. Once they have the thing they came for, attention drops.

Email 2 (day 2): Address the most common objection to buying the main offer
Email 3 (day 3): Social proof or case study — specific, verifiable, with a name and result
Email 4 (day 5): Urgency, direct ask, or close

## AGENT OPERATING PRINCIPLES

When building any email sequence or campaign, the agent must:

1. **Follow the core email format** — reward for opening, one concept, contextual CTA, PS statement — on every single email
2. **Design for deliverability** — plain text, one to two links, no money language in body, always set preview text
3. **Make unsubscribing easy** — never make it hard, a clean engaged list beats a large disengaged one
4. **Segment before sending** — never send the same email to buyers and non-buyers, advanced and beginners, or different stages
5. **Apply the give-to-ask ratio** — deposit Goodwill before every campaign, never ask without earning the right
6. **Default to the challenge format** for quarterly campaigns — it satisfies the value equation and warms cold contacts faster than any other format
7. **Always test on 10–15% first** — never blow the full list on an untested campaign
8. **Wrap seasonally** — the same offer can run every quarter with a different name and framing
9. **Build the welcome sequence to create engagement immediately** — ask for the reply before delivering the lead magnet
10. **Optimise for CTR over open rate** — click-through rate is the real measure of email performance, not opens
11. **Always include a PS statement** — it is the second most read part of the email and should never be left blank
12. **Apply timing recommendations** — B2C on Monday/Tuesday, B2B on Wednesday, 10am–12pm or 1pm–3pm send windows
